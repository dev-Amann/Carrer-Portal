CHAPTER 1 – INTRODUCTION

  • Background:

In today’s competitive world, choosing the right career has become a challenging task for students and job seekers. With the rapid growth of industries and the emergence of new professions, individuals often face confusion about which career path best matches their skills and interests. Many students complete their education without proper career guidance and later realize a mismatch between their abilities and the jobs they pursue.

Traditional career counseling methods, though helpful, are often generic, time-consuming, and unable to provide personalized recommendations. This lack of proper guidance can lead to dissatisfaction, underutilization of skills, and limited career growth. At the same time, employers continuously seek candidates with the right skills and competencies, which creates a gap between job seekers and industry expectations.

To overcome this challenge, there is a strong need for a system that can assist individuals in making informed career decisions. A skill-based recommendation approach addresses this by analyzing a person’s existing skills, highlighting areas of improvement, and suggesting suitable career paths. Such a solution not only helps students plan their future effectively but also empowers job seekers to upskill themselves and align with industry requirements.

The Skill-Based Career Recommendation Portal is designed to fill this gap by providing a platform where users can explore personalized career options, understand their skill gaps, and gain proper guidance for professional growth.

  • Objective:

The main objective of the Skill-Based Career Recommendation Portal is to assist students and job seekers in making informed career choices by analyzing their skills and interests. The specific objectives are:

To provide personalized career recommendations based on the user’s existing skills, interests, and background.

To identify skill gaps by comparing user skills with the requirements of various career paths.

To suggest relevant learning resources that can help users bridge their identified skill gaps.

To enable career comparison so users can evaluate multiple options in terms of salary, growth opportunities, skill requirements, and demand levels.

To allow bookmarking and report generation for saving and exporting career recommendations in a structured format.

To facilitate expert consultations by connecting users with verified professionals for career guidance.

To ensure secure and user-friendly interaction between users, experts, and administrators.

To provide administrators with insights and statistics on career demand, skill gaps, and user engagement through reports and dashboards.

  • Need of Project:

1.3.1 Purpose: The primary purpose of this project is to guide students and job seekers in making informed career choices by providing personalized recommendations based on their skills and interests. It aims to reduce confusion, bridge the gap between education and industry expectations, and help individuals identify suitable career paths along with the skills required to succeed in them.

1.3.2 Scope: The system provides users with career recommendations, skill gap analysis, and learning resources to enhance employability. It allows users to compare different careers, bookmark suitable options, and generate reports for future reference. Additionally, it enables consultation with verified experts to gain professional advice. The scope also extends to administrators, who can manage experts, monitor platform usage, and analyze trends through dashboards.

1.3.3 Applicability: This portal can be applied in multiple domains:

Educational Institutions: To support students in career planning and guidance.

Training and Skill Development Centers: To suggest career-oriented learning paths.

Job Seekers and Professionals: To explore new opportunities and identify required upskilling.

By addressing the needs of learners, professionals, and institutions, the system has wide applicability in both academic and professional environments.

CHAPTER 2: SURVEY OF TECHNOLOGIES

The development of the Skill-Based Career Recommendation Portal required a careful selection of modern, efficient, and scalable technologies. A web application of this nature is composed of several distinct layers: the frontend (client-side), the backend (server-side), the database, and various third-party services that provide specialized functionality. This chapter provides a comprehensive survey of the technologies chosen for each layer of the application, along with a comparative analysis and justification for their selection. The goal was to build a technology stack that is robust, maintainable, and well-suited to the project's specific requirements.

Frontend Technologies

The frontend is the user-facing part of the application, responsible for rendering the user interface (UI) and managing user interactions. A dynamic and responsive UI is critical for engaging users.

React.js

React.js is a popular open-source JavaScript library developed by Facebook for building user interfaces, particularly for Single Page Applications (SPAs).

Comparative Analysis: Other popular frontend frameworks include Angular and Vue.js. While Angular is a comprehensive MVC framework and Vue.js is known for its simplicity, React was chosen for its component-based architecture. This approach allows for the creation of reusable UI components (like forms, buttons, and career cards), which simplifies development and maintenance. React's virtual DOM also ensures high performance by minimizing direct manipulation of the actual DOM, leading to a faster and smoother user experience, which is essential for a data-rich application like this portal.

Justification: For a portal that requires dynamic updates—such as displaying career recommendations, filtering results, and updating charts in the admin dashboard—React's state management and component lifecycle methods are highly effective. Its vast ecosystem, including libraries like Axios for API calls and Recharts for data visualization, made it the ideal choice.

Tailwind CSS and Bootstrap 5

Tailwind CSS is a utility-first CSS framework for rapidly building custom user interfaces, while Bootstrap is a component-based framework.

Justification: This project leverages a hybrid approach. Bootstrap 5 was used for its pre-built components like modals and forms to quickly set up the basic structure and ensure responsiveness. Tailwind CSS was then used for fine-grained styling and creating a custom, modern design without writing extensive custom CSS files. This combination provides both speed of development and design flexibility.

Backend Technologies

The backend is the server-side logic that powers the application, handling everything from business logic and database interactions to security.

Python and Flask

Python is a high-level, interpreted programming language known for its readability and extensive libraries. Flask is a lightweight "micro" web framework for Python.

Comparative Analysis: A major alternative to Flask is Django, another Python-based framework. Django is a "batteries-included" framework that provides an admin panel, ORM, and other features out of the box. However, for this project, which is primarily an API-driven service, Flask's minimalism and flexibility were more suitable. It allows for the creation of a clean, custom backend API without unnecessary overhead.

Justification: Python was the language of choice due to its strong data processing capabilities, making it perfect for implementing the career recommendation algorithm. Flask provides the necessary tools to build a secure and RESTful backend API that serves data to the React frontend. Extensions like Flask-CORS (for handling cross-origin requests) and Flask-Mail (for email functionality) are easily integrated, making it a powerful yet simple choice.

Database Technology

MySQL

MySQL is a widely-used, open-source relational database management system (RDBMS).

Comparative Analysis: Alternatives include other SQL databases like PostgreSQL or NoSQL databases like MongoDB. A NoSQL database might be suitable for unstructured data, but the data in this project—user profiles, skills, careers, and their relationships—is highly structured and relational.

Justification: MySQL was chosen for its reliability, performance, and strong data integrity features. Using a relational model with foreign key constraints ensures that the relationships between users, their skills, and their bookmarked careers are consistently maintained. Its widespread adoption means excellent community support and compatibility with backend languages like Python through connectors.

Third-Party Services and APIs

To enhance functionality without building every feature from scratch, several third-party APIs were integrated.

Jitsi Meet

Jitsi Meet is an open-source video conferencing platform. It was chosen for the "Expert Consultation" feature because it can be easily and securely embedded into the web application via an iframe. This provides a seamless user experience, as neither the user nor the expert needs to create a separate account or install any software.

Razorpay

For processing consultation fees, the Razorpay API was selected. It is a leading payment gateway in India, known for its developer-friendly integration, robust security, and support for multiple payment methods. Its clear documentation allowed for a straightforward implementation of a secure payment workflow.

Gmail SMTP with Flask-Mail

For the "Email Career Report" feature, the standard Gmail SMTP server was used in conjunction with the Flask-Mail library. This is a cost-effective and highly reliable solution for sending automated emails from the application server, requiring minimal configuration.

Supporting Libraries

jsPDF / react-to-print: To enable the PDF download feature, a client-side library like jsPDF was chosen. This allows the user's browser to generate the PDF directly, reducing the load on the server.

Recharts: For the admin dashboard, Recharts was selected as the charting library. It is a composable charting library built on React components, making it incredibly easy to create interactive and visually appealing bar, pie, and line charts to display user engagement and other statistics.

3.1 Problem Definition

In today's dynamic and competitive job market, students and professionals often face significant hurdles in identifying and pursuing a suitable career path. The lack of a centralized, intelligent, and personalized guidance system leads to several critical problems that hinder effective career planning.

The primary problems this project addresses are:

Lack of Personalized Guidance: Career advice is often generic and fails to consider an individual's unique skills, interests, and experience. This makes it difficult for users to find career paths that genuinely align with their profile.

Unclear Skill Requirements: Many individuals are unaware of the specific skills needed to enter or progress in a particular field. This "skill gap" often becomes a significant barrier to employment and career growth.

Fragmented Information: Key career-related information—such as salary benchmarks, market demand, required tools, and learning resources—is scattered across countless websites, making research a tedious and often incomplete process.

Limited Access to Expert Advice: Direct, one-on-one mentorship from industry experts is a crucial but often unavailable or expensive resource, leaving many to navigate their career decisions without professional validation.

This project aims to solve these issues by creating a unified web portal that provides data-driven career recommendations, performs skill gap analysis, suggests targeted learning resources, and facilitates direct connections with vetted experts.

4.2.3 Security Issues

This section identifies potential security vulnerabilities in the Skill-Based Career Recommendation Portal and proposes detailed mitigation strategies to ensure data integrity, confidentiality, and the overall security posture of the application.

1. SQL Injection (SQLi)

Description: As the backend architecture relies on a MySQL database, the application is susceptible to SQL Injection attacks. This vulnerability arises when user-provided data—such as from skill input forms, login fields, or search parameters—is incorporated directly into SQL queries without proper sanitization. An attacker could submit crafted input (e.g., ' OR '1'='1' --) to manipulate the query logic. A successful attack could lead to unauthorized access to all data in the database, data modification or deletion, and in some cases, remote code execution on the database server.

Mitigation:

Use Parameterized Queries: The primary defense is to use parameterized queries (also known as prepared statements) with the mysql-connector-python library. This approach separates the SQL code from the data, ensuring that user input is always treated as literal data and not as executable code.

Leverage an ORM: Implementing an Object-Relational Mapping (ORM) tool like SQLAlchemy is highly recommended. ORMs abstract database interactions and automatically use parameterized queries, significantly reducing the risk of SQLi.

Strict Server-Side Validation: All user-supplied input must be validated on the server-side. This includes checking for expected data types, lengths, and formats. Client-side validation is useful for user experience but provides no security.

2. Cross-Site Scripting (XSS)

Description: The application displays user-generated content in various places, such as user profiles, expert details, and saved career notes. If this data is not properly encoded before being rendered, an attacker can inject malicious client-side scripts. These scripts would then execute in the browsers of other users who view the content, potentially leading to session hijacking, defacement of the website, or redirection to malicious sites. This is particularly critical for the expert profile section, where a malicious expert could inject a script that affects all users viewing their profile.

Mitigation:

Contextual Output Encoding: Ensure that React components properly escape all data rendered to the DOM. While JSX provides default encoding against XSS, developers must remain cautious when using properties like dangerouslySetInnerHTML, which should be avoided whenever possible.

Implement a Content Security Policy (CSP): A strong CSP should be implemented via HTTP headers. This policy tells the browser which sources of content (scripts, styles, images) are trusted, effectively preventing the execution of unauthorized scripts.

Backend Sanitization: Before storing any user input that will be displayed, use a library on the Flask backend (like bleach in Python) to sanitize the input, removing any potentially malicious HTML and script tags while allowing a safe subset if needed.

3. Insecure Direct Object References (IDOR)

Description: An IDOR vulnerability occurs when the application uses a direct reference to an object (like a user ID, booking ID, or saved career ID) and fails to verify if the current user is authorized to access it. For example, a user might access their saved career report via a URL like /api/reports/user/123. An attacker could manipulate this URL, changing 123 to 124, to potentially access the report of another user.

Mitigation:

Mandatory Access Control Checks: For every request that accesses a resource, the Flask backend API must perform a check to verify that the currently authenticated user has the necessary permissions. The logic should be: "Does the resource with resource_id belong to the user_id associated with the current session?"

Use Indirect References: Avoid using direct database identifiers (like auto-incrementing integers) in URLs. Instead, use non-sequential, hard-to-guess identifiers like UUIDs (Universally Unique Identifiers) for each resource. This makes it computationally infeasible for an attacker to guess the IDs of other objects.

4. Cross-Site Request Forgery (CSRF)

Description: CSRF is an attack that tricks an authenticated user into submitting a malicious request to the application. If a user is logged into the portal, an attacker could lure them to a different, malicious website. This website could contain hidden code (e.g., an HTML form that auto-submits) that sends a request to your portal to perform a state-changing action, like changing the user's password or booking an expert consultation, using the victim's active session.

Mitigation:

Implement Anti-CSRF Tokens: The standard defense is the Synchronizer Token Pattern. The server generates a unique, secret token for each user session and embeds it in forms. When a request is submitted, the server verifies that the token included in the request matches the one stored for the session. Flask extensions like Flask-WTF can manage this process automatically. This ensures that only requests originating from the application's own forms are processed.

5. Insecure Third-Party Integrations

Description: The portal integrates with external services like Razorpay for payments and Jitsi Meet for video calls. These integrations introduce potential risks. API keys or secrets hardcoded in the frontend React code could be easily discovered by an attacker. Furthermore, if the application blindly trusts callbacks or webhooks (e.g., payment success notifications from Razorpay), an attacker could spoof these requests to gain unauthorized access to services.

Mitigation:

Secure Credential Storage: All API keys, secrets, and other sensitive credentials must be stored exclusively on the backend server, ideally as environment variables or using a secrets management system. They must never be exposed to the client-side React application.

Verify Webhook Signatures: For services like Razorpay that use webhooks, it is critical to verify the cryptographic signature included in each webhook request. This ensures that the request is authentic and originated from the trusted third party, not an imposter.

6. Authentication and Session Management Weaknesses

Description: Flaws in how user authentication and sessions are managed can lead to account takeovers. This includes not enforcing strong password policies, storing passwords insecurely, not properly invalidating sessions upon logout or password change, and having excessively long session timeouts.

Mitigation:

Strong Password Policies: Enforce minimum password length and complexity requirements on the client and server side.

Secure Password Hashing: User passwords must never be stored in plaintext. Use a strong, salted, and adaptive hashing algorithm like bcrypt to store password hashes securely in the database.

Robust Session Management: Use secure, HTTP-only session cookies to prevent access via client-side scripts. Sessions must be properly invalidated on the server-side during logout, and session identifiers should be regenerated after a user successfully authenticates to prevent session fixation attacks. Implement a reasonable session timeout period (e.g., 30 minutes of inactivity).

Brute-Force Protection: Implement rate limiting and account lockout mechanisms on the login endpoint to thwart automated password-guessing attacks.

Authentication Module

The authentication module manages user registration, login, and token management. The registration process involves OTP verification, password hashing with bcrypt, and JWT token issuance.

User Registration with OTP Verification:

@auth_bp.route('/register', methods=['POST'])

def register():

"""Register a new user (requires OTP verification first)"""

data = request.get_json()

name = data.get('name', '').strip()

email = data.get('email', '').strip().lower()

password = data.get('password', '')

otp = data.get('otp', '').strip()

# Validate required fields

return jsonify({'success': False, 'error': 'All fields are required'}), 400

if not is_valid:

return jsonify({'success': False, 'error': error_message}), 400

# Verify OTP

otp_success, otp_message = verify_otp(email, otp, purpose='verification')

if not otp_success:

return jsonify({'success': False, 'error': otp_message}), 400

# Hash password and create user

password_hash = hash_password(password)

Password hashing utility:

def hash_password(password):

"""Hash password using bcrypt with 12 salt rounds"""

salt = bcrypt.gensalt(rounds=12)

hashed = bcrypt.hashpw(password.encode('utf-8'), salt)

return hashed.decode('utf-8')

def verify_password(password, password_hash):

"""Verify password against bcrypt hash"""

return bcrypt.checkpw(password.encode('utf-8'), password_hash.encode('utf-8'))

JWT Token Verification

The verify_token decorator ensures route protection by decoding and validating JWT tokens, checking for expiry and privileges:

def verify_token(required_admin=False):

"""Decorator to verify JWT token and protect routes"""

def decorator(f):

@wraps(f)

def decorated_function(*args, **kwargs):

auth_header = request.headers.get('Authorization')

if not auth_header:

return jsonify({

'success': False, 'error': 'Authorization header is missing',

'code': 'MISSING_TOKEN'

}), 401

parts = auth_header.split()

if len(parts) != 2 or parts[0].lower() != 'bearer':

return jsonify({

'success': False,

'error': 'Invalid authorization header format',

'code': 'INVALID_HEADER'

}), 401

token = parts[1]

payload = decode_token(token)

if payload.get('type') != 'access':

return jsonify({

'success': False, 'error': 'Access token required',

'code': 'INVALID_TOKEN_TYPE'

}), 401

if required_admin and not payload.get('is_admin', False):

return jsonify({

'success': False, 'error': 'Admin privileges required',

'code': 'INSUFFICIENT_PRIVILEGES'

}), 403

# Store user info in Flask's g object

g.user_id = payload.get('user_id')

g.is_admin = payload.get('is_admin', False)

g.expert_id = payload.get('expert_id')

g.role = payload.get('role')

return f(*args, **kwargs)

return decorated_function

return decorator

Skill Gap Analysis

The analysis compares user skills with career requirements, calculating gaps and readiness percentages:

@staticmethod

def calculate_skill_gap(user_id, career_id):

"""Calculate skill gaps between user skills and career requirements"""

career = g.db.query(Career).get(career_id)

user_skills = g.db.query(UserSkill).filter_by(user_id=user_id).all()

user_skill_map = {us.skill_id: us.proficiency for us in user_skills}

career_skills = g.db.query(CareerSkill).options(

joinedload(CareerSkill.skill)

).filter_by(career_id=career_id).all()

gaps = []

met_requirements = []

proficiency_levels = ['beginner', 'intermediate', 'advanced', 'expert']

for cs in career_skills:

req_idx = proficiency_levels.index(cs.required_level.lower())

if cs.skill_id not in user_skill_map:

gaps.append({

'skill_name': cs.skill.name,

'current_level': 'Not acquired',

'required_level': cs.required_level,

'gap': req_idx + 1

})

else:

user_idx = proficiency_levels.index(user_skill_map[cs.skill_id].lower())

if user_idx < req_idx:

gaps.append({

'skill_name': cs.skill.name,

'current_level': user_skill_map[cs.skill_id],

'required_level': cs.required_level,

'gap': req_idx - user_idx

})

else:

met_requirements.append({

'skill_name': cs.skill.name,

'current_level': user_skill_map[cs.skill_id],

'required_level': cs.required_level

})

readiness = (len(met_requirements) / len(career_skills) * 100)

return {

'career_title': career.title,

'gaps': gaps,

'met_requirements': met_requirements,

'readiness_percentage': round(readiness)

}
